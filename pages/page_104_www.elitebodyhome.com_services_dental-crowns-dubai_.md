## Steps For Dental Crown Procedure
Getting a dental crown usually takes two visits, with a few steps involved. The objective is to condition the impacted tooth and place a crown that visually and functionally resembles your other natural teeth.
##### Consultation
Your dentist will examine the affected tooth with X-rays or other diagnostic tools to decide whether a crown is the best treatment option.
##### Tooth Preparation
The tooth is formed to receive the crown. This could include a removal of some of the outer wall of the tooth.
##### Impressions
Your dentist makes impressions of the prepared tooth and sends them to a dental lab to create a custom crown.
##### Temporary Crown
A temporary crown is placed to cover up the hole while waiting for your permanent crown to be made.
## Benefits
Like many dental procedures dental crowns provide a variety of benefits and are among the most frequently recommended restorative treatments in dentistry.
##### Protection
They shield weak or damaged teeth from further decay, fractures, or breakage.
##### Durability
Crowns are constructed from durable materials such as porcelain, ceramic, or metal, making them long-lasting solution.
##### Aesthetic Improvement
Crowns can be designed to mimic the coloration and shape of your natural teeth, making your smile look its best.
##### Restored Function
Crowns return the ability to chew and speak comfortably if tooth damage affected those functions.
## Eligibility
Not all dental conditions call for a crown, but they are perfect when a tooth is significantly compromised or weakened. People that qualify for dental crowns usually have:
##### Very Decayed or Damaged Teeth
Crowns are typically recommended when fillings or other restorative options are insufficient.
##### Root Canal-Treated Teeth
After this procedure, the tooth can lose strength and become brittle, which is why a crown is needed for protection.
##### Cracked or Broken Teeth
Crowns protect the tooth from more damage by covering the entire tooth structure.
##### Worn-Down Teeth
But crowns can protect teeth that have become worn down from grinding or erosion from acid.
## Preparation
There are several steps with dental crowning therapy, before receiving a crowning therapy. Having the right approach ahead of time will help not just to make the procedure work but also to promote long-lasting results.
##### Consultation with your dentist
Talk about your symptoms, goals and medical history.
##### X-Rays & Imaging
Your dentist will probably take X-rays to evaluate the tooth and surrounding bone structure.
##### Dental Hygiene
Practice good oral hygiene leading up to the procedure to help prevent your risk of infection or complications.
##### Understanding the procedure
Your dentist will go over the procedure for you, as well as what you can expect, the timeline, and aftercare instructions.
##### Temporary Crown Care
If they put in a temporary crown, then avoid sticky foods and brush soft to protect the temporary ‚Äî it will come out if you eat the wrong things.

## Frequently Asked Questions
### How long do dental crowns last?
Dental crowns typically last between 10 to 15 years, but with proper care, some crowns can last even longer.
### Do crowns look like natural teeth?
Yes, dental crowns are custom-made to match the size, shape, and color of your surrounding teeth, providing a natural appearance.
### What materials are used for crowns?
Crowns can be made from a variety of materials, including porcelain, ceramic, metal alloys, or a combination. Porcelain and ceramic are often chosen for front teeth because they closely mimic natural tooth enamel.
### Are dental crowns painful?
The procedure itself is typically painless due to local anesthesia, though some patients may experience mild discomfort after the procedure, which usually subsides within a few days.
